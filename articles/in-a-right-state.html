In This Week's Issue...
 



I've never met the guy but I imagine he has a pretty good memory - and his golf isn't too shabby either.

And according to his fellow professionals, what separates Tiger Woods from the pack is not just his amazing ability to control a golf ball, but his incredible power of concentration.

His ability to stay calm and focused and perform - under pressure.

His ability to control his mental state.

Now if, like me, you're regularly beaten at crazy golf by small children and people with guide dogs, then you may have no delusions of becoming the world's next golfing superstar.

But learning to access certain mental states can accelerate your success both academically and in business.

Because research has shown that specific mental states can dramatically improve your ability to learn and recall information.

So much so that Dominic O'Brien, 8 times world memory champion, not only trains his memory but also devotes time to developing his ability to switch - at will - into a 'peak performance' state.

What's more, many of the world's fastest readers - such as Sean Adam who was clocked at 3,850 words per minute (that's more than 15 times faster than Joe average!) - attribute much of their success to being able to slip into the same highly resourceful mental states.

So what's the secret?

Well, it's all to do with your brain waves.

Researchers have long known that our brain waves fluctuate between 4 distinct patterns, each characterised by the number of Cycles Per Second (CPS) -

Beta - 14-30 CPS - normal consciousness

Alpha - 7-14 CPS - relaxed alertness

Theta - 3-7 CPS - deep relaxation

Delta - 1-3 CPS - deep sleep

What's intriguing is that two of these brain wave patterns hold the key to 'learning how to learn' quickly and effectively.

Alpha and Theta.

They are also both associated with relaxation.

And it all makes sense really.

Ever tried to remember something under pressure?

The harder you try to summon up the information, the more elusive it becomes and the more frustrated you get. Then later, lying in the bath, nice and relaxed, it pops back into your head.

Stress can play havoc with your memory and leave you in a right state. Which is precisely the WRONG state for learning and recall.

In contrast, when you relax your brain's activity drops from Beta to Alpha or even Theta - and brain wave patterns in the left and right hemispheres become more synchronised - an optimal state for study and learning.

So simply practising relaxation techniques, particularly prior to a study session, can boost your storage and recall of information.

Try it and see.

There are many resources available on relaxation techniques from simple breathing exercises through to more advanced forms of meditation.

You can also now get biofeedback machines that hook up to your PC and allow you to actually see your brain wave patterns - and even train your ability to control them.

However you do it, the key is simply to relax...deeply and regularly.

The Final Word: Don't get in a right state the next time you need to study or can't recall something, relax and put yourself in the right state instead.
